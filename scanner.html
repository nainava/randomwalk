Random Walk: Finder

The atlas proves the pattern exists — breakthroughs emerge when someone carries an idea from one field into another. But the atlas is retrospective. This tool detects random walks while they’re happening. Three automated channels scan live research data for cross-pollination signals. A fourth channel takes nominations from people who recognize the pattern in real time.

Channel 01

Cross-Citation Scraper

When a paper in field A cites a paper from unrelated field B, that’s a bibliometric fingerprint of cross-pollination. A neuroscience paper citing materials science. A CS paper citing evolutionary biology. These are the textual traces of someone carrying an idea across a boundary.

The mechanism that produced optogenetics (algae biology → neuroscience), AlphaFold (game AI → structural biology), and cisplatin (electrochemistry → oncology).
Channel 02

Border-Crossers

Researchers whose recent papers are in a different field than their historical body of work. When someone’s home expertise doesn’t match what they’re publishing now, they’re carrying knowledge across a boundary.

Eng was a VA hormone researcher who presented Gila monster venom work at the American Diabetes Association conference. That border-crossing produced GLP-1 drugs.
Channel 03

Failed Experiment Residuals

Terminated clinical trials that didn’t hit their primary endpoint — but documented unexpected observations in adverse events or secondary outcomes. “Failure” is defined relative to the original goal, but the residual has real properties.

Post-it Notes came from a failed adhesive. Viagra from a failed blood pressure drug. Teflon from a failed refrigerant. This channel systematizes what those discoveries left to chance.
Channel 04

Open Nominations

The discoveries in our atlas all started with someone working at an unexpected intersection. Know someone doing that right now? It takes three minutes.

Self-nominations welcome. Name, or a link to their work.
Their background and the new domain they’re applying it to.
2–3 sentences. What insight or technique from field A are they bringing to field B?
Casually interested or intensely committed? How long? Have they published, presented, or built anything at the intersection?
What could it lead to?

No account required. Submissions are reviewed monthly.
