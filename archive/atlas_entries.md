# Random Walk: Atlas

*Where investment in X led to discovery in Y*

---

A VA endocrinologist orders lizard venom from a catalog. A Hungarian biochemist sews cash into a teddy bear to flee a country that cut her funding. A graduate student in Spain spends a decade getting papers rejected about bacterial DNA repeats nobody cares about. A team at Google tries to make a translation product faster. These acts of randomness have given us weight-loss drugs, mRNA vaccines, CRISPR gene editing, and generative AI. Random walks can change the world.

We traced 11 of these chains in full — every person, every institution, every funding source, every moment the discovery almost died. Then we outlined 40 more. In all 51 cases, the original funding was allocated for a purpose unrelated to the eventual discovery. $181 million in research funding, spent on everything from lizard venom studies to refrigerant chemistry, accidentally produced $15.8 trillion in value. The median return on the unintended outcome was 200,000x the original investment.

Every discovery in this dataset delivered what was funded. Eng did hormone research. Google Brain improved translation. Pfizer ran cardiovascular trials. The funded work succeeded on its own terms. But the knock-on effects dwarfed the intended returns. Hormone research became $100B in weight loss drugs. A translation improvement became the entire AI revolution. A failed angina trial became Viagra. Cold War communications became the internet. The unintended discovery was almost always more valuable than the intended one.

This isn’t an accident — it’s a structural feature of research funding. Intensity in one domain creates knowledge that’s useful in domains nobody anticipated. The multiplier is real, it’s measurable in the data, and it’s being systematically undervalued.

The multiplier increases when the cutting room floor is shared. Every chain below has near-death moments where findings almost disappeared — patents refused, papers rejected, researchers defunded. 10 of 11 breakthroughs had at least one moment where an institution explicitly said no. Each of those moments nearly zeroed out a multiplier worth billions. Sharing failures, preserving anomalous results, and making the cutting room floor accessible to other fields is the cheapest way to increase the multiplier.

Fund more. Fund weird. Share everything. The yield compounds.

## Discovery Chains (11 deep entries)

### 001. GLP-1 Weight Loss Drugs (Ozempic, Wegovy, Mounjaro)

**Date:** Scientific chain: 1967–2024; First FDA approval (exenatide): 2005; Semaglutide for obesity: 2021  
**Key Figures:** Chain began with Joel Habener (GLP-1 discovery), John Eng (exendin-4 isolation), and was commercialized through Amylin Pharmaceuticals and later Novo Nordisk/Eli Lilly  
**Initial Funding:** U.S. Veterans Administration (VA) and National Institutes of Health (NIH)  
**Funding Reason:** Basic research into venom peptides as probes for mammalian physiology; diabetes research at VA hospitals  
**Current Use:** Treatment of type 2 diabetes and obesity; active investigation for Alzheimer's disease, addiction, NASH, and cardiovascular disease  
**Chain Length:** 12 steps · 57+ years

**X (What was funded):** NIH-funded basic research into Gila monster lizard venom peptides, conducted at a VA hospital with catalog-ordered reptiles

**Y (What was discovered):** A $100+ billion global market for GLP-1 obesity drugs, with emerging applications in Alzheimer's disease, addiction, and metabolic disorders

In the 1980s, a VA endocrinologist named John Eng, working in a small laboratory at the Bronx Veterans Affairs Medical Center, ordered Gila monster venom from a biological supply catalog on a hunch that reptile venoms might contain peptides that mimic or illuminate human gut hormones. After years of painstaking isolation work, Eng identified a peptide he called exendin-4, which turned out to be a near-perfect mimic of the human hormone GLP-1 — but dramatically more stable. When Eng tried to patent the discovery through the VA, the institution declined. Eng patented it himself, presented a poster at an ADA conference, and a small San Diego biotech saw it. Thirty years later, 9% of the U.S. population is projected to be on GLP-1 drugs by the end of the decade.

#### The Chain

**1967–1983** — Rosalyn Yalow, Joel Habener, Graeme Bell  
*Bronx VA; Massachusetts General Hospital; University of Chicago*  
Yalow's VA radioimmunoassay enabled gut hormone detection; Habener cloned proglucagon, discovering GLP-1.

> GLP-1 emerged not from diabetes research but from gene sequencing done with no therapeutic target in mind.

*Funding: NIH; Veterans Administration*

**1986–1987** — Jens Juul Holst; Svetlana Mojsov / Joel Habener  
*University of Copenhagen; Massachusetts General Hospital*  
GLP-1 stimulates insulin only when blood sugar is elevated — theoretically impossible to cause dangerous hypoglycemia.

> This glucose-dependent safety profile reframed GLP-1 from biological curiosity to therapeutic target.

*Funding: Danish Medical Research Council; NIH; Novo Nordisk*

**1990–1992** — Dr. John Eng  
*Bronx Veterans Affairs Medical Center*  
Ordered Gila monster venom from a catalog; hypothesized an animal eating few times yearly evolved extreme metabolic peptides.

> Eng isolated exendin-4 — 53% homology to GLP-1, resistant to degradation, far longer half-life than any mammalian equivalent.

*Funding: Veterans Administration*

**1993–1994** — Dr. John Eng  
*Bronx VA; Washington D.C.*  
VA declined to patent exendin-4; Eng filed the patent out of pocket and presented findings as a conference poster.

> Had Eng not personally filed, exendin-4 enters the public domain with no commercialization pathway. The VA's refusal accidentally preserved the innovation.

*Funding: Personal funds*

**1996** — Andrew Young, Amylin Pharmaceuticals  
*ADA Annual Meeting; San Diego*  
Young saw Eng's poster, recognized exendin-4's stability, licensed the patent to begin development as exenatide.

> A single conference poster was the entire commercialization mechanism — no formal tech transfer, no institutional deal.

*Funding: Amylin Pharmaceuticals venture capital*

**1999–2005** — Amylin Pharmaceuticals; Eli Lilly; FDA  
*San Diego; Indianapolis; Silver Spring MD*  
Amylin nearly went bankrupt before Eli Lilly partnership rescued trials; FDA approved exenatide (Byetta) April 2005.

> Weight loss appeared as an unexpected secondary finding in trial data — the 'side effect' that would become a hundred-billion-dollar indication.

*Funding: Eli Lilly co-development agreement*

**2000–2021** — Lotte Bjerre Knudsen, Novo Nordisk; STEP trial investigators  
*Denmark; global trial sites*  
Fatty acid modification enabled once-weekly dosing; STEP 1 showed 14.9% body weight reduction — unprecedented without surgery.

> STEP trials reframed obesity as a biological disease with a pharmaceutical solution, triggering a global paradigm shift.

*Funding: Novo Nordisk internal R&D*

**2023–present** — NIH National Institute on Aging; Novo Nordisk  
*Copenhagen; London; Baltimore*  
GLP-1 receptors found widely in the brain; users showed lower Alzheimer's rates; Phase 3 trials launched.

> NIH-funded venom peptide biology now funds brain disease trials — the same programs that mapped peptide receptors in the 1970s underpin the Alzheimer's hypothesis.

*Funding: NIH; Novo Nordisk; ARPA-H*

### 002. The Transformer / Modern AI

**Date:** June 12, 2017 (arXiv preprint); NeurIPS 2017  
**Key Figures:** Ashish Vaswani, Noam Shazeer, Niki Parmar, Jakob Uszkoreit, Llion Jones, Aidan N. Gomez, Lukasz Kaiser, Illia Polosukhin (Google Brain / Google Research)  
**Initial Funding:** Google LLC (internal research budget, Google Brain team)  
**Funding Reason:** Improve Google Translate's neural machine translation quality and computational efficiency  
**Current Use:** Foundation of virtually every large language model: GPT-4, Claude, Gemini, LLaMA, Mistral, and thousands of derivative systems  
**Chain Length:** 14 steps · ~70 years

**X (What was funded):** The need to improve Google Translate — a commercial product serving hundreds of millions of users daily, funded by Google's advertising revenue

**Y (What was discovered):** The Transformer architecture: the universal substrate of the modern AI revolution, enabling GPT, BERT, Claude, Gemini, and the entire large language model ecosystem

In 2017, a team of eight researchers at Google published a 15-page paper titled 'Attention Is All You Need' with the modest goal of making machine translation faster. They had no idea they were writing the foundational document of a technological revolution. But the lineage runs much deeper — through two devastating AI winters, multiple funding collapses, and decades of academic unfashionability. The chain runs from McCulloch and Pitts in 1943, through the brutal Minsky-Papert critique that nearly killed the field, through backpropagation's revival, through LSTMs, through attention mechanisms — and finally to eight engineers who were just trying to make a translation product work better.

#### The Chain

**1943–1958** — Warren McCulloch, Walter Pitts; Frank Rosenblatt  
*University of Chicago; Cornell Aeronautical Laboratory*  
McCulloch and Pitts modeled the neuron mathematically; Rosenblatt built the first trainable network for the U.S. Navy.

> Rosenblatt added a learning rule — the first proto-gradient-descent, the seed of everything that followed.

*Funding: U.S. Office of Naval Research*

**1969** — Marvin Minsky and Seymour Papert  
*MIT, Cambridge, Massachusetts*  
Published 'Perceptrons,' proving single-layer nets couldn't solve XOR — neural network funding collapsed for over a decade.

> Neural network research became professionally toxic. Graduate students were advised against the field.

*Funding: N/A — DARPA dramatically reduced neural network grants*

**1974–1986** — Paul Werbos; David Rumelhart, Geoffrey Hinton, Ronald Williams  
*Harvard; UC San Diego; Carnegie Mellon*  
Rumelhart, Hinton, and Williams published backpropagation in Nature, making multi-layer networks trainable for the first time.

> Backpropagation provided the missing ingredient for deep networks and triggered an explosion of renewed interest.

*Funding: NSF; DARPA; Sloan Foundation*

**1997** — Sepp Hochreiter and Jürgen Schmidhuber  
*IDSIA, Lugano; TU Munich*  
Published Long Short-Term Memory, solving the vanishing gradient problem with gating mechanisms.

> LSTMs kept neural networks alive through the second AI winter — without them, no trained researchers exist to build what came next.

*Funding: Swiss National Science Foundation*

**2006–2012** — Geoffrey Hinton, Yann LeCun, Yoshua Bengio; Krizhevsky, Sutskever, Hinton  
*University of Toronto; NYU; Université de Montréal*  
CIFAR united the three researchers during the AI winter; AlexNet won ImageNet 2012 by a margin that ended all debate.

> AlexNet trained on two $500 GPUs crushed every competitor. Every major tech company immediately began aggressively hiring deep learning researchers.

*Funding: CIFAR; NSERC; Canadian Foundation for Innovation*

**2014–2015** — Sutskever, Vinyals, Le; Bahdanau, Cho, Bengio  
*Google Brain; Université de Montréal*  
Seq2seq compressed sentences into fixed vectors; Bahdanau attention let the decoder dynamically weight each input word instead.

> Bahdanau attention is the direct ancestor of self-attention in the Transformer. The core idea survived essentially unchanged into 2017.

*Funding: Google LLC; CIFAR; NSERC*

**2016–2017** — Google NMT team; Ashish Vaswani et al.  
*Google Brain, Mountain View and New York*  
GNMT deployed to 500M daily users but was too slow to parallelize; Vaswani's team eliminated recurrence entirely with multi-head self-attention.

> The question 'how do we make Google Translate faster?' accidentally produced the substrate of the entire modern AI revolution.

*Funding: Google LLC*

**2018–2024** — OpenAI, Google DeepMind, Anthropic, Meta AI, hundreds more  
*San Francisco; London; Mountain View; globally*  
BERT, GPT-1 through GPT-4, Claude, Gemini, LLaMA — every major AI system built on the Transformer.

> A 1943 theoretical neuron paper → two near-extinction AI winters → eight engineers fixing a translation product → the most consequential architecture of the 21st century.

*Funding: Microsoft invested $13B in OpenAI; aggregate AI investment exceeded $500B by 2024*

### 003. mRNA Vaccines

**Date:** Core discovery: 2005 (modified mRNA); COVID-19 application: 2020–2021  
**Key Figures:** Katalin Karikó and Drew Weissman (key discovery); commercialized via Moderna and BioNTech  
**Initial Funding:** Hungarian Academy of Sciences (1978–1985); later NIH and American Heart Association grants (repeatedly rejected)  
**Funding Reason:** Basic research into RNA biology and gene therapy; not specifically targeted at vaccines  
**Current Use:** COVID-19 vaccines; pipeline for influenza, RSV, HIV, cancer immunotherapy, and rare genetic diseases  
**Chain Length:** 13 steps · 42 years

**X (What was funded):** Decades of rejected grants, institutional demotion, and basic biochemistry research into how messenger RNA interacts with the innate immune system — work considered a scientific dead end

**Y (What was discovered):** Two COVID-19 vaccines authorized in under a year, protecting hundreds of millions of people and demonstrating a universal platform for rapid vaccine development

In 1978, a young Hungarian biochemist named Katalin Karikó became captivated by the idea that synthetic mRNA could instruct human cells to produce their own medicines. For more than two decades, nearly every institution, grant committee, and peer review panel told her she was wrong. She lost her faculty position at UPenn, was demoted, had her salary cut, and watched her NIH grant applications fail year after year. In 2005, collaborating with Drew Weissman, she discovered that substituting pseudouridine for uridine in synthetic mRNA allowed it to slip past the immune system's sentinels. That single insight — published in Immunity and largely ignored — unlocked mRNA as a therapeutic platform. When SARS-CoV-2 emerged in January 2020, both Moderna and BioNTech had optimized mRNA platforms ready. Pfizer-BioNTech received FDA authorization 326 days after the virus's genome was sequenced.

#### The Chain

**1978–1985** — Katalin Karikó  
*Hungarian Academy of Sciences, Szeged*  
Early RNA-lipid delivery research; state funding cut; smuggled $900 in a teddy bear and emigrated.

> Without this desperate emigration, the research stops entirely.

*Funding: Hungarian Academy of Sciences*

**1989–1995** — Katalin Karikó, UPenn administration  
*University of Pennsylvania*  
Every NIH grant application rejected; UPenn demoted Karikó, cut her salary, told her career was over — same year she was diagnosed with cancer.

> Most researchers would have left. Karikó stayed, supporting herself on other scientists' projects while continuing mRNA work on the side.

*Funding: Reduced UPenn salary; no independent grants*

**1997–1998** — Katalin Karikó and Drew Weissman  
*University of Pennsylvania*  
Met at a photocopier in the UPenn library and agreed to collaborate with no dedicated funding.

> A chance encounter at a photocopier initiated the collaboration that would solve the central problem of mRNA therapeutics.

*Funding: Weissman's NIH HIV vaccine grants (adjacent)*

**2005** — Katalin Karikó and Drew Weissman  
*University of Pennsylvania*  
Substituting pseudouridine for uridine prevents immune rejection of synthetic mRNA — published in Immunity, largely ignored.

> The immune system's rejection was not fundamental but an artifact of unmodified nucleosides. One paper unlocked the entire field.

*Funding: NIH (Weissman lab)*

**2010** — Derrick Rossi; Robert Langer, Noubar Afeyan  
*Harvard Stem Cell Institute; Boston*  
Rossi used modified mRNA to reprogram adult cells into stem cells, saw platform potential, co-founded Moderna.

> A stem cell biologist read an immunology paper ignored by pharma and immediately understood it as a platform technology.

*Funding: Flagship Pioneering*

**2013–2019** — Uğur Şahin, Özlem Türeci (BioNTech); Barney Graham, Jason McLellan (NIAID); Moderna  
*Mainz, Germany; NIH Bethesda; Cambridge MA*  
BioNTech hired Karikó as SVP; NIAID developed prefusion spike stabilization; Moderna ran Phase I trials.

> Two independent threads — modified mRNA and spike protein stabilization — matured in parallel, ready to be combined the moment a new pathogen appeared.

*Funding: BioNTech private capital; NIAID intramural; Moderna/BARDA*

**January 2020 – December 2020** — Moderna; Pfizer-BioNTech; FDA  
*Cambridge MA; Mainz Germany; global trial sites*  
Within 48 hours of genome release, both companies designed vaccine candidates; Phase III showed 94–95% efficacy; FDA authorized both vaccines by December 18.

> Forty-two years after Karikó began RNA work in Szeged, the technology she refused to abandon was authorized as the fastest vaccine in human history.

*Funding: Operation Warp Speed; US government advance purchase agreements*

### 004. The Internet

**Date:** 1969 (ARPANET first link); 1983 (TCP/IP switch-over); 1991 (World Wide Web public release)  
**Key Figures:** No single inventor — key figures include Paul Baran, Donald Davies, Lawrence Roberts, Vint Cerf, Bob Kahn, and Tim Berners-Lee  
**Initial Funding:** U.S. Department of Defense Advanced Research Projects Agency (ARPA)  
**Funding Reason:** Fear that a Soviet nuclear first strike could destroy centralized U.S. command-and-control communications  
**Current Use:** Global infrastructure connecting ~5.4 billion people, underpinning commerce, communication, science, and culture  
**Chain Length:** 14 steps · ~40 years

**X (What was funded):** ARPA funding to survive nuclear decapitation of U.S. command communications

**Y (What was discovered):** The World Wide Web and the modern Internet used by over half of humanity

In the late 1950s, U.S. military planners were terrified that a Soviet ICBM strike could sever all command-and-control communications in a single blow. ARPA funded research into a network with no center. That idea — packet switching — was invented independently by Paul Baran at RAND and Donald Davies at NPL, became ARPANET, evolved into the Internet through TCP/IP, and was transformed by Tim Berners-Lee's World Wide Web — originally a document management hack at a physics lab. A weapon against nuclear annihilation became the connective tissue of civilization.

#### The Chain

**1957–1964** — Eisenhower administration; Paul Baran  
*Pentagon; RAND Corporation, Santa Monica*  
ARPA founded after Sputnik; Baran proposed chopping messages into addressed blocks through a distributed mesh to survive nuclear attack.

> AT&T declared it 'impossible and unnecessary.' Their president told Baran it couldn't work and they didn't want it. The project was shelved.

*Funding: U.S. Air Force / ARPA*

**1965–1969** — Donald Davies (NPL); Lawrence Roberts (ARPA); Frank Heart (BBN)  
*Teddington UK; Washington D.C.; Cambridge MA*  
Davies coined 'packet switching' independently; Roberts synthesized both approaches into ARPANET's design; BBN delivered IMP #1 to UCLA August 29, 1969.

> UK government declined to fund Davies's national network — the architecture migrated to America, where ARPA had money and urgency.

*Funding: UK Ministry of Technology; ARPA*

**October 29, 1969** — Charley Kline, Leonard Kleinrock; Bill Duvall at SRI  
*UCLA Boelter Hall → Stanford Research Institute*  
Kline typed 'LOGIN'; the system crashed after two letters. The first message in Internet history was 'LO.'

*Funding: ARPA*

**1971** — Ray Tomlinson  
*BBN Technologies, Cambridge*  
Combined a mailbox program with file transfer, chose the @ symbol, sent the first email — then told a colleague not to mention it because ARPA hadn't paid for it.

> By 1972, email was 75% of all network traffic. Nobody predicted it. An unsanctioned side project became the first killer app.

*Funding: BBN ARPA contract (self-directed work)*

**1973–1983** — Vint Cerf, Bob Kahn; Jon Postel  
*Stanford; ARPA; USC*  
Cerf and Kahn designed TCP/IP to connect incompatible networks; January 1 1983 'Flag Day' forced mandatory cutover from NCP.

> The OSI model, backed by ISO, ITU, and every European government, was the official rival standard. TCP/IP won because it was free, open, and already running.

*Funding: ARPA; DARPA*

**1985–1991** — NSF; Tim Berners-Lee (CERN)  
*Washington D.C.; CERN, Geneva*  
NSFNet became the backbone after ARPANET was decommissioned; Berners-Lee submitted a proposal his supervisor annotated 'Vague but exciting' and built HTML, HTTP, URLs, and the first web server.

> Gopher was more popular in 1992–93, then the University of Minnesota announced licensing fees. The Web was free. Gopher collapsed overnight.

*Funding: NSF; CERN internal budget*

**1992–1995** — Marc Andreessen, Eric Bina (NCSA); Jim Clark; NSF  
*University of Illinois; Mountain View CA*  
Andreessen built Mosaic on $6.85/hour, driving 341,634% Web traffic growth; co-founded Netscape, which IPO'd with stock hitting $75 on day one; NSF lifted the Acceptable Use Policy the same year.

> Microsoft had missed the Internet entirely. Gates's 'Internet Tidal Wave' memo reversed course. The commercial Internet era began.

*Funding: NSF; Kleiner Perkins*

### 005. CRISPR Gene Editing

**Date:** 2012 (landmark Science paper); 1987 (initial observation); 2023 (first FDA-approved therapy)  
**Key Figures:** Jennifer Doudna, Emmanuelle Charpentier (key 2012 paper); Francisco Mojica (foundational discovery); Feng Zhang (first mammalian cell application)  
**Initial Funding:** Japanese Ministry of Education (Ishino, 1987); Spanish Ministry of Education / University of Alicante (Mojica, 1990s)  
**Funding Reason:** Basic microbiology research into E. coli DNA replication; ecology of salt-tolerant microorganisms in Mediterranean saltpans

Then Jennifer Doudna and Emmanuelle Charpentier realized the bacterial machinery could be reprogrammed as a universal genetic cut-and-paste tool. In 2023 the FDA approved the first CRISPR therapy — 36 years after Ishino's footnote.

### 006. Penicillin

**Date:** Discovery: September 1928; First therapeutic use: 1941; Mass production: 1943–1944  
**Key Figures:** Alexander Fleming (discovery), Howard Florey, Ernst Chain, and Norman Heatley (therapeutic development)  
**Initial Funding:** British War Office / Medical Research Committee — World War I wound infection research  
**Funding Reason:** Catastrophic mortality from infected battlefield wounds in WWI  
**Current Use:** Penicillin and derivatives remain among the most widely prescribed antibiotics globally; annual antibiotic market exceeds $40 billion  
**Chain Length:** 11 steps · 26 years

**X (What was funded):** British War Office funding for WWI wound-infection bacteriology research at St. Mary's Hospital, London

**Y (What was discovered):** The world's first mass-produced antibiotic, saving an estimated 200 million lives in the 20th century

The story of penicillin is not one flash of genius but a 16-year chain of accidents, neglect, wartime desperation, and transatlantic collaboration. Fleming's 1928 contaminated petri dish was real but unactionable — he couldn't purify the compound, and the medical establishment ignored his paper for a decade. It restarted only when Florey and Chain at Oxford, funded by the Rockefeller Foundation, picked it up in 1939. Their first patient nearly died when they ran out of drug. Florey flew to America with mold in his coat lining. A lab assistant found the highest-yielding mold in history on a Peoria cantaloupe. By D-Day, enough penicillin existed to treat every Allied wounded soldier.

#### The Chain

**1914–1918** — Alexander Fleming, Almroth Wright  
*Western Front; St. Mary's Hospital, London*  
Watched antiseptics kill white blood cells faster than bacteria in infected wounds.

*Funding: British War Office; Medical Research Committee.*

**1928** — Alexander Fleming  
*St. Mary's Hospital, London*  
Returned from vacation; Penicillium mold had drifted onto a staph dish, leaving a clear kill zone.

> Fleming couldn't purify or stabilize it. His 1929 paper was largely ignored; he stopped pursuing it by 1931.

*Funding: Medical Research Council; St. Mary's Hospital.*

**1939** — Howard Florey, Ernst Chain, Norman Heatley  
*Sir William Dunn School of Pathology, Oxford*  
Found Fleming's paper in a literature search; Heatley built extraction apparatus from milk churns.

> May 25, 1940: all untreated mice died; all penicillin-treated mice survived.

*Funding: Rockefeller Foundation; MRC; Nuffield Trust.*

**1941** — Florey, Heatley, Chain; Albert Alexander (patient)  
*Radcliffe Infirmary, Oxford; then USA*  
First human patient's fever broke in 24 hours — then they ran out of drug, recycled penicillin from his urine, and he died. Florey flew to America with mold spores rubbed into his coat lining.

> ⚠ NEAR-DEATH MOMENT: Alexander's death proved the drug worked but Oxford's micro-batch production was wholly inadequate. Only American industrial scale could save it.

*Funding: MRC emergency wartime grant; Rockefeller Foundation; US OSRD.*

**1943** — Mary K. Hunt ('Moldy Mary')  
*USDA NRRL, Peoria, Illinois*  
Found a cantaloupe at a local market yielding 200x more penicillin than Fleming's strain.

> After mutagenesis, 1,000x yield. Virtually all penicillin today descends from that cantaloupe.

*Funding: USDA NRRL; War Production Board.*

**1943–1944** — Pfizer, Merck, Squibb, Eli Lilly  
*Brooklyn; Rahway; Indianapolis*  
Pfizer converted a Brooklyn ice plant into a fermentation facility under War Production Board contracts.

> By June 1944: 650 billion units per month. By D-Day, enough to treat every wounded Allied soldier.

*Funding: War Production Board guaranteed procurement; US Army Surgeon General.*

### 008. Lithium for Bipolar Disorder

**Date:** FDA approved October 14, 1970  
**Key Figures:** John Frederick Joseph Cade (clinical discovery, 1948); Mogens Schou (clinical validation, 1950s–1960s)  
**Initial Funding:** Repatriation Mental Hospital, Bundoora, Victoria, Australia (state public hospital budget)  
**Funding Reason:** Investigating a metabolic toxin hypothesis — that manic patients produced excess uric acid causing symptoms  
**Current Use:** First-line mood stabilizer for bipolar disorder; suicide prevention; antidepressant augmentation  
**Chain Length:** 8 steps · 22 years

**X (What was funded):** John Cade's hypothesis that mania was caused by a urinary toxin, tested in a repurposed hospital kitchen

**Y (What was discovered):** The first effective pharmacological treatment for a major psychiatric disorder — delayed 22 years in the US because lithium is unpatentable

Working in a repurposed kitchen at a mental hospital with almost no resources, Australian psychiatrist John Cade injected guinea pigs with urine from manic patients. To make uric acid soluble for injection, he used lithium urate — and noticed the guinea pigs became unexpectedly calm. He tested lithium on himself first, then on patients. The results were dramatic. Yet for two decades no pharmaceutical company invested in proper trials because lithium cannot be patented. Danish psychiatrist Mogens Schou ran the rigorous trials that finally convinced the FDA in 1970.

#### The Chain

**1948** — Dr. John Cade  
*Repatriation Mental Hospital, Bundoora, Australia*  
Injecting manic patients' urine into guinea pigs; used lithium urate to dissolve uric acid for injection.

> Guinea pigs given lithium urate became unexpectedly calm — the lithium itself was the sedative. Cade tested the dose on himself for weeks before treating patients.

*Funding: No external funding; Cade worked in a repurposed kitchen with equipment he bought himself.*

**1948–1949** — Dr. John Cade and 10 manic patients  
*Repatriation Mental Hospital, Bundoora*  
Administered lithium carbonate to 10 manic patients; all showed striking remission.

> Patients hospitalized for years were discharged. Published in Medical Journal of Australia, 1949 — and largely ignored outside Australia.

*Funding: Repatriation Mental Hospital.*

**1950s–1969** — Pharmaceutical industry (collective inaction)  
*USA and Europe*  
Declined to fund trials; lithium is a naturally occurring element that cannot be patented.

> ⚠ NEAR-DEATH MOMENT: A drug used widely in Australia and Denmark was entirely unavailable in the US for over two decades due to the patent gap.

*Funding: None.*

**1954–1970** — Dr. Mogens Schou  
*Aarhus University Psychiatric Hospital, Denmark*  
Ran first randomized double-blind trials of lithium; proved prophylactic effect against both mania and depression.

> Fought sustained opposition from British psychiatry for years; his decades of advocacy built the evidence base that finally moved the FDA.

*Funding: Aarhus University; Danish Medical Research Council; Lundbeck Foundation.*

**1970** — U.S. FDA; Ronald Fieve (Columbia University)  
*Washington, D.C.*  
FDA approved lithium carbonate for acute mania — 22 years after Cade's first observations.

> Lithium remains on the WHO Essential Medicines List and is one of the only psychiatric drugs proven to reduce suicide risk.

*Funding: NIMH grants (Fieve).*

*Wilmington, Delaware*

*Paris, France*  
Grégoire coated fishing tackle with Teflon; Colette suggested applying it to cooking pans.

*Funding: Self-funded; later private investment in Tefal.*

**1960s–present** — DuPont, NASA, Bob Gore  
*Global*  
PTFE expanded into aerospace heat shields, joint replacements, Gore-Tex (1969), semiconductor manufacturing.

*Funding: Commercial R&D and government contracts.*
