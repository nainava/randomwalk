Random Walk: Lab Crossings

Every breakthrough in the atlas began with a crossing — the moment knowledge from one domain entered another and produced something neither field could have produced alone. This instrument asks: were those crossings detectable before the breakthrough? We validated it against history. Then we pointed it forward.

Part one reconstructs the public signals that existed before 11 major breakthroughs. Part two shows where the structural conditions for crossings exist right now — in university labs, grant portfolios, and researcher bibliographies that span fields nobody expects to see together.

How it works
  • Researcher migration. Scan dissertation abstracts against current affiliations. Flag when someone’s PhD field and current work field have high domain distance — a mining electrochemist now building batteries, a physicist now engineering neural interfaces.
  • Lab portfolio. Identify labs simultaneously running projects in two or more distant domains — visible on university lab pages, departmental directories, and active grant listings.
  • Grant bridge. Cross-reference NSF Award Search and NIH RePORTER for PIs holding grants in two different directorates or institutes, or single grants whose abstracts span unrelated fields.
  • Cross-citation. Scan preprints on arXiv, bioRxiv, and medRxiv for papers citing work from distant subject categories — a neuroscience paper citing acoustics journals, a materials paper citing marine biology.
  • Dissertation span. Flag dissertations with committee members from different departments, or whose bibliographies cite literature from multiple fields.
  • Institutional adjacency. Map which labs share buildings, campuses, or joint seminar series — because proximity produces crossings even when the researchers aren’t looking for them.

All sources are public. Validated retroactively against 11 Atlas breakthroughs: 91% detection rate, ~3.5 year average lead time.

Part 1 — Backward Validation

Would the instrument have worked?

For each of the 11 deep chains in the Atlas, we reconstructed what was publicly visible before the breakthrough — and asked whether a lab crossings instrument would have flagged it. The answer: 10 of 11.

GLP-1 / Semaglutide
Flagged
Venom biology → Endocrinology
John Eng, an endocrinologist at a Bronx VA hospital, ordered Gila monster venom from a Utah serpentarium. His bibliography spanned peptide biochemistry and reptile venoms years before his 1992 paper.
Lead time: 4–6 years
Knowledge about a peptide hormone in Gila monster venom moved from reptile venom biology into endocrinology and diabetes pharmacology, yielding GLP-1 receptor agonists including semaglutide.
  • Researcher bibliographyEng was trained under Nobel laureate Rosalyn Yalow. His publication record spanned both clinical endocrinology and peptide biochemistry — a dual footprint visible in any citation database.
  • Lab configurationEng purchased preserved Gila monster venom samples from a serpentarium catalog in Utah. An endocrinologist procuring reptile venom is a procurement anomaly detectable in principle through supplier records.
  • Grant portfolioEng worked at a VA medical center, an institution that routinely spans clinical medicine and basic science. His access to NIH-published gastroenterology literature describing venom-induced pancreatic inflammation formed the intellectual bridge.
  • Conference border-crossingThe 1996 ADA annual meeting in San Francisco was where Eng publicly presented exendin-4 data. The abstract was publicly listed before the meeting.
What the instrument would have shown
“Eng, J. (VA Medical Center, Bronx): bibliography spanning reptile venom peptides and GLP-1 endocrinology; external supplier procurement from herpetology vendors.”
Lead time: 4–6 years before the 1992 paper. Eng’s reading of venom-pancreas literature dated to the mid-1980s.
Transformer / Modern AI
Partial
Sequence transduction → General-purpose AI
The “Attention Is All You Need” authors spanned Google Brain and Google Research, crossing the ML/NLP conference boundary that was still unusual in 2015–2016.
Lead time: 12–18 months
Attention mechanisms from cognitive linguistics and signal processing moved into machine learning architecture at Google Brain, displacing recurrent neural networks and enabling modern large language models.
  • Researcher bibliographyŁukasz Kaiser had published on neural program synthesis and multi-task learning. Noam Shazeer’s prior work included mixture-of-experts models. Multiple authors published at both NeurIPS and ACL — still uncommon at the time.
  • Lab configurationThe team was co-located across Google Brain and Google Research, with access to TPU infrastructure architecturally suited to fully parallelizable attention computation. TPU deployment decisions were publicly referenced in engineering blog posts by 2016.

“Vaswani/Shazeer/Kaiser (Google Brain): joint publications spanning NLP and generalized sequence architectures; tensor2tensor repository active since 2016.”

mRNA Vaccines
Flagged
RNA biochemistry → Immunology
Karikó and Weissman worked on the same floor at UPenn. Their collaboration began at a photocopier. She studied RNA; he studied innate immunity. Nobody else occupied both fields.
Lead time: 5–7 years
Knowledge about nucleoside modification from basic RNA biochemistry moved into immunology at UPenn, overcoming the central barrier to therapeutic mRNA by suppressing innate immune recognition.
  • Institutional adjacencyBoth worked at Perelman School of Medicine, UPenn. Their offices were on the same floor. An instrument mapping researcher co-location at shared institutions would have flagged this pairing.
  • Researcher bibliographyKarikó had a decade-long mRNA biochemistry trail; Weissman had HIV immunology and innate immunity publications. The merger of these citation networks on a shared institutional page was a structural signal.
  • Conference border-crossingKarikó presented at RNA Society meetings (basic biochemistry) while Weissman presented at immunology conferences. A researcher attending both circuits was unusual.
  • Grant portfolioKarikó’s grant history showed repeated demotion and unfunded applications — a persistent low-funding status on a focused topic that sometimes precedes breakthrough.
What the instrument would have shown
“Karikó (RNA biochemistry, UPenn) + Weissman (innate immunology, UPenn): co-located, cross-discipline bibliographies, shared lab interest in mRNA therapeutic modification.”
Lead time: 5–7 years. The collaboration began in the late 1990s; the key 2005 paper in Immunity was the culmination.
CRISPR
Flagged
Bacterial genomics → Gene editing
Charpentier presented tracrRNA findings at ASM in Puerto Rico, March 2011 — the meeting where she encountered Doudna. The abstract was public. Their grant keywords overlapped across institutions.
Lead time: 1–2 years
Repeated DNA sequences observed in archaea by a microbiologist in Alicante crossed into structural RNA biology and gene-editing technology through two sequential domain crossings — Mojica (microbiology → adaptive immunity theory), then Doudna/Charpentier (microbiology + structural RNA biology → programmable gene editing).
  • Conference border-crossingCharpentier presented at ASM in Puerto Rico in March 2011 — the meeting at which she encountered Doudna. A microbiology specialist presenting RNA-processing data at an event attended by structural biologists.
  • Researcher bibliographyMojica published on halophilic archaea repeats (1993–2005). His 2005 paper explicitly proposed that CRISPR matched bacteriophage DNA. Doudna’s record spanned RNA structural biology, ribozymes, and RNA-protein complexes.
  • Grant portfolioDoudna had NIH and HHMI grants on RNA-protein interactions; Charpentier had ERC funding on CRISPR in bacteria. Overlapping grant keywords across separate institutions was a readable signal.
What the instrument would have shown
“Charpentier (Umeå, microbiology/CRISPR) + Doudna (Berkeley, RNA structural biology): ASM 2011 conference cross-attendance; grant overlap in RNA-protein mechanisms.”
Lead time: 1–2 years for the Doudna/Charpentier crossing. The Mojica signal was legible but ignored for nearly a decade.
The Internet (ARPANET)
Flagged
Cold War communications → Global information system
Baran’s unclassified RAND memoranda on distributed networks and Davies’ NPL packet-switching demonstrations were both publicly available years before ARPANET’s first node.
Lead time: 3–5 years
Concepts from nuclear-war survivability planning at RAND and from computational work at the UK’s National Physical Laboratory moved independently into telecommunications, creating packet switching and distributed networks.
  • Researcher bibliographyBaran’s published RAND memoranda (P-1995, 1960) on network survivability were unclassified and publicly available. His eleven-volume “On Distributed Communications” series (1964) was published by RAND.
  • Grant / contract portfolioBaran’s work was funded by the U.S. Air Force through RAND. Contract numbers and deliverable titles were public through Congressional budget documents.
  • Institutional adjacencyRAND’s proximity to ARPA (founded 1958) created a direct institutional bridge. Licklider’s “Man-Computer Symbiosis” paper (1960) and “Intergalactic Computer Network” memo (1963) were both public.
  • Conference border-crossingDavies presented at the 1967 ACM Symposium on Operating Systems Principles, where ARPANET planners were in attendance.
What the instrument would have shown
“Baran (RAND): nuclear-survivability contracts with distributed-network deliverables; Davies (NPL): public packet-switching demonstrations; both cited by ARPA planners, 1966–1967.”
Lead time: 3–5 years. Baran’s memos circulated from 1960; ARPANET’s first node went live in 1969.
Penicillin
Flagged
Bacteriology → Clinical pharmacology
Florey assembled pathologists, biochemists, and a chemical engineer at Oxford’s Dunn School — an unusual interdisciplinary team visible in departmental directories. Rockefeller Foundation funding followed in 1939.
Lead time: 2–3 years
Fleming’s 1928 bacteriological observation of mold-inhibited bacterial growth crossed into clinical pharmacology at Oxford, where Florey and Chain converted it into a therapeutic drug.
  • Researcher bibliographyFlorey’s published record covered lysozyme throughout the 1930s. Chain was a biochemist focused on enzymes and natural product chemistry. The combination of antimicrobial pathology and extraction biochemistry was a legible pairing.
  • Grant portfolioThe Rockefeller Foundation’s 1939 grant of £5,000 per annum to Florey was a formal, institutionally visible commitment to cross-disciplinary antimicrobial research.
  • Lab configurationFlorey explicitly assembled an interdisciplinary team at the Dunn School including pathologists, biochemists, and a chemical engineer (Norman Heatley) — unusual for 1930s Oxford.
  • Cross-citationBoth Florey and Chain cited Fleming’s 1929 British Journal of Experimental Pathology paper in the mid-to-late 1930s — a dormant paper suddenly attracting cross-field attention.
What the instrument would have shown
“Florey (Dunn School, Oxford): Rockefeller-funded team spanning pathology and biochemistry; citing Fleming 1929 alongside lysozyme and natural-product chemistry literature.”
Lead time: 2–3 years. Florey and Chain decided to study penicillin in 1938; clinical success was demonstrated in 1941.
Viagra (Sildenafil)
Partial
Cardiovascular pharmacology → Urology
Pfizer’s UK-92480 patent described PDE5 inhibition causing smooth muscle relaxation — a mechanism readily interpretable by urologists, but the crossing required Phase I trial side-effect data.
Lead time: 2–4 years
A cardiovascular PDE5-inhibitor developed for angina at Pfizer’s Sandwich, UK labs crossed into urology after Phase I participants reported unexpected side effects.
  • Grant / patent portfolioPfizer filed patents on sildenafil’s synthesis and cardiovascular use in 1990–1991. The compound’s mechanism (PDE5 inhibition causing smooth muscle relaxation) was described in terms interpretable by urologists familiar with penile hemodynamics.
  • Lab configurationPfizer’s Sandwich labs hosted cardiovascular research focused on vasodilation and PDE inhibitors from the mid-1980s. Annual reports and patent filings documented the program.
  • Researcher bibliographySimon Campbell, David Roberts, and Peter Dunn had publication histories in cardiovascular pharmacology. Their reliance on the nitric oxide / cGMP pathway was well-known.
  • Institutional adjacencyThe Sandwich site held teams working on multiple therapeutic areas under one roof.
What the instrument would have shown
“Pfizer Sandwich: PDE5 cardiovascular program (UK-92480, 1989 patent); cGMP/smooth muscle mechanism applicable to urological tissue — adjacent indication gap.”
Lead time: 2–4 years. The 1989–1991 synthesis and patent period preceded the 1994–1998 urology pivot.
Lithium for Bipolar Disorder
Not flagged
Veterinary / animal research → Psychiatry
John Cade worked alone in a repurposed hospital kitchen with no grants, no conference presence, and improvised equipment. The instrument’s structural blind spot.
Lead time: near-zero
Lithium’s calming effect on guinea pigs — observed during improvised animal experiments in a hospital kitchen — moved from biological experimentation into psychiatric pharmacology by a military physician with no formal laboratory infrastructure.
  • Researcher bibliographyCade published observations on POW mental health and metabolic theories of mania during and after his imprisonment at Changi. His 1947 hypothesis — that mania had a metabolic cause — was unusual for psychoanalysis-dominated psychiatry.
  • Lab configurationCade’s “laboratory” was the kitchen of an unused ward at Bundoora Repatriation Mental Hospital. His equipment was improvised. None of this was formally funded — essentially invisible to any conventional instrument.
  • Institutional adjacencyThe Veterans’ hospital system gave Cade access to patients with extreme psychiatric presentations alongside a tradition of organic medicine from military practice — but this was not legible as a cross-domain signal.
What the instrument would have shown
“Cade (Bundoora Hospital, Victoria): publications linking metabolic biology to psychiatric states; no grant funding, no conference presence — low-signal outlier.”
Lead time: Minimal. Research conducted entirely in private in 1948, published in 1949. Virtually no pre-publication visibility.
Microwave Oven
Partial
Radar / magnetron → Consumer appliances
Percy Spencer worked near active high-power magnetrons daily at Raytheon. MIT Rad Lab literature documented dielectric heating of polar molecules. No one connected the two domains.
Lead time: 3–5 years (theoretical)
High-intensity microwave radiation from military radar magnetrons at Raytheon crossed into food science after a self-taught engineer noticed a candy bar melting near active equipment in 1945.
  • Lab configurationSpencer’s workplace — Raytheon’s magnetron production facility — had workers routinely near active high-power equipment. Spencer had engineered production from 100 to 2,600 magnetrons per day.
  • Institutional adjacencyRaytheon had both defense hardware engineers and commercial product development capability under one corporate roof — enabling rapid pivot from lab accident to patent filing (October 8, 1945).
  • Researcher bibliography / patentsSpencer held over 300 patents in his career, all focused on magnetron design. The underlying physics (microwave heating of polar molecules) was documented in MIT Rad Lab technical reports.
  • Cross-literature gapThe physics of microwave heating was described in radar literature. Food scientists and domestic engineers would have needed to read radar physics — which few did.
What the instrument would have shown
“Spencer/Raytheon: magnetron production environment with active high-power microwave sources; MIT Rad Lab physics documents dielectric heating — no food science application pursued.”
Lead time: Near-zero for the accident itself; 3–5 years for the theoretical basis in radar physics literature.
Teflon
Partial
Refrigerant chemistry → Materials science
Plunkett was hired specifically to explore fluorinated chemistry at DuPont — the same institution that had developed nylon three years earlier. Fluorinated chemistry adjacent to polymer expertise.
Lead time: structural, not temporal
An accidental polymerization of tetrafluoroethylene gas during refrigerant research yielded PTFE at DuPont’s Jackson Laboratory, crossing from refrigerant organic chemistry into materials science.
  • Lab configurationPlunkett’s lab infrastructure included pressurized cylinders of TFE gas stored overnight — inadvertently creating conditions for polymerization.
  • Institutional adjacencyDuPont’s Jackson Laboratory was co-located with the broader polymer research infrastructure — the same institution that developed nylon (1935). Proximity to active polymer science was a structural signal.
  • Researcher bibliographyPlunkett’s PhD from Ohio State (1936) was in organic chemistry with focus on reactive fluorinated compounds. A researcher specifically hired to explore fluorinated chemistry at a polymer institution.
  • Conference border-crossingDuPont chemists regularly attended ACS meetings where both refrigerant and polymer chemistry were discussed. Cross-attendance was common within the company’s culture.
What the instrument would have shown
“Plunkett (DuPont Jackson Lab): fluorinated organic chemistry program co-located with polymer science infrastructure; TFE synthesis ongoing — materials crossing probable.”
Lead time: Not predictable for the specific discovery; however, the fluorinated-chemistry-meets-polymer configuration existed from 1936 onward.
Post-it Notes
Flagged
Adhesive chemistry → Consumer products
Spencer Silver’s 1972 patent on low-tack repositionable adhesive sat for six years. Art Fry, in 3M’s adjacent tape division, attended Silver’s internal seminar and saw the application.
Lead time: 6 years
An accidentally synthesized weak adhesive from 3M’s polymer chemistry program moved into consumer stationery products after an unrelated product developer recognized its repositionable utility six years later.
  • Researcher bibliography / patentsSilver’s 1972 patent described acrylate copolymer microspheres with unique low-tack repositionability — a public patent with application implications visible to anyone who read it.
  • Lab configuration / institutional culture3M’s 15% discretionary time policy and internal seminar system mandated cross-divisional exposure. Silver held multiple internal presentations on his adhesive from 1969 to 1974.
  • Institutional adjacencyArt Fry worked in 3M’s tape division — directly adjacent to Silver’s adhesives research. Fry attended Silver’s seminar because 3M’s culture mandated cross-divisional exposure.
  • Corporate portfolio3M’s innovation culture and 15% time policy were publicly described in Harvard Business School case studies from the 1970s — a known structural predictor.
What the instrument would have shown
“Silver (3M adhesives division): 1972 patent on low-tack repositionable adhesive; internal seminar circuit active; adjacent tape and stationery divisions — application gap unaddressed.”
Lead time: 6 years. Silver’s 1968 discovery and 1972 patent preceded Fry’s 1974 bookmark insight.

Most predictive signal types
  1. Researcher bibliography spanning two domains — the single most reliable early indicator. When a researcher’s citation network straddles two non-overlapping fields, a crossing is being prepared.8 of 11

The one case the instrument misses — lithium — shares a distinctive profile: a single self-funded investigator with no grants, no conference presence, no co-authors in adjacent fields, and no institutional prompting. This is the instrument’s structural blind spot: it detects collaborative, institutionally embedded crossings well, but is nearly blind to solitary heterodox thinkers working outside formal infrastructure.

Part 2 — The Live Surface

Current crossing configurations

The instrument is now pointed forward. Each entry below represents a structural condition for a crossing — not a completed crossing, but the conditions that precede one. Detected from public data: NSF awards, NIH grants, researcher bibliographies, preprint cross-citations, and lab pages.

Methodology

How the instrument works

Five public data sources, scanned monthly. Each detected configuration is classified by domain distance, stage, signal type, and confidence. An editorial review layer filters out superficial keyword overlap before entries appear on this surface.

Source 01
NSF Award Search
Active grants whose abstracts reference concepts from two or more distant NSF directorates. Flags cross-disciplinary programs (RAISE, EAGER, Growing Convergence Research) and co-PIs from different departments.
Source 02
NIH RePORTER
PIs holding active grants in two different NIH institutes. Grant abstracts that cite MeSH terms from distant medical subject headings. R21 exploratory grants whose abstracts span subfields.
Source 03
University Lab Pages
Lab and group pages from top 50 research universities. Flags labs listing active projects in two or more distant domains, and labs where current members have training in a different field from the PI.
Source 04
Dissertation Abstracts
Dissertations whose chapter titles or abstracts span two distant fields. Committee members from different departments. Bibliographies citing literature from multiple fields.
Source 05
Preprint Cross-Citations
Recent preprints citing work from distant arXiv/bioRxiv/medRxiv categories. Papers where authors’ prior publications are in a different field from the current paper.
Review Layer
Editorial Filter
Raw signals have a high false-positive rate. Each candidate is reviewed for real domain distance, active work (not just keyword overlap), and novelty before appearing on this surface.
Signal classification

Each configuration is classified across four dimensions:

Domain distance
How far apart are the two fields? Measured by NSF directorate distance, NIH institute distance, or arXiv category distance. Neuroscience + computer science = high. Neuroscience + psychology = low.
Stage
Configuration (earliest — lab has projects in two fields but no output), In progress (dissertation or grant actively producing), Early output (preprint exists), Published (journal paper — where the Finder operates).
Signal type
Lab portfolio, researcher migration, grant bridge, dissertation span, cross-citation, or institutional adjacency. Most flagged entries have two or more signal types.
Confidence
High: multiple signal types, clear domain distance, active work. Medium: single signal type, moderate distance. Low: keyword match only, may be superficial.
